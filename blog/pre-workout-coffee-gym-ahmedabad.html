Coffee before a workout: what the evidence actually says

The sports science on caffeine and performance is not contested. Caffeine at 3–6mg per kilogram of bodyweight — consumed 30 to 45 minutes before exercise — consistently improves endurance, increases peak power output, sharpens reaction time, and delays the onset of perceived fatigue. These are not marginal gains. Studies across cycling, weightlifting, and sprint performance show measurable improvements across all three.

For a 70kg person, the target dose is 210–420mg of caffeine. A double espresso made from 100% Arabica delivers approximately 100–120mg. That puts you in the lower-to-mid range of the effective window — enough to perform, not enough to crash or overstimulate. It is also the cleanest delivery format available. No beta-alanine tingle. No artificial colouring. No ingredient list you need a biochemistry degree to read.

Section 02 · Why espresso specifically

Why espresso — not cold brew, not instant

Not all coffee delivers caffeine at the same speed or with the same cleanness. The format and the bean both matter.

Cold brew — great for mornings, wrong for pre-workout

Cold brew has high caffeine per cup — sometimes higher than espresso when measuring per 250ml. But cold brew is a sustained-release format. The large liquid volume, the slower digestion of cold liquids, and the way cold extraction processes caffeine all mean it enters the bloodstream more gradually. That makes it excellent for a steady-state morning focus session. It is the wrong tool for a sharp, immediate pre-workout window. You want the caffeine spike timed to your first working set, not spread across two hours.

Instant — cheaper, but inconsistent

Instant coffee typically uses Robusta-dominant blends — higher in caffeine by weight, but also higher in bitterness compounds and often with additives and fillers. The caffeine content varies significantly between brands and even between batches. For daily gym use, that inconsistency matters. You want to know exactly what you are putting in, especially when you are training at 6am on a Tuesday.

Espresso, pulled fresh — the right answer

100% Arabica espresso, pulled fresh, delivers clean and consistent caffeine with a narrower jitter profile than Robusta blends. The smaller volume (30–60ml) means faster gastric emptying and faster absorption. And because Arabica's lower chlorogenic acid content produces a smoother, less bitter cup, you are not fighting the taste on an empty pre-workout stomach. It is the format built for this use case.

Section 04 · Post-workout recovery

Cold brew post-workout: the recovery drink nobody talks about

Post-workout, your body needs hydration first. But for the afternoon that follows a 6am session — work by 9am, back-to-back meetings until lunch — a second caffeine hit is usually required, and the timing actually works.

Cold brew is the right tool for this moment. Cold brew is naturally lower in acid than hot espresso — the 12-hour cold extraction process draws less of the acidic compounds out of the bean. That makes it significantly gentler on a stomach that has just been put through an hour of physical stress. The caffeine is still there. The smoothness is better. The stomach does not object.

Frequently asked questions

Is coffee actually a good pre-workout, or is this just marketing?+
It is not marketing — it is some of the most consistently replicated sports science available. Caffeine at 3–6mg/kg bodyweight is established in peer-reviewed research across cycling, weightlifting, sprint, and endurance events. A double espresso delivers approximately 100–120mg, which for most adults sits in the lower-to-mid effective range. It improves endurance, increases power output, sharpens focus, and delays perceived fatigue.
How much caffeine is in a P.S. double espresso?+
Approximately 100–120mg from 100% Arabica. This varies slightly by dose and extraction, but because we will use a consistent recipe and consistent beans, the cup-to-cup variation is low. That consistency is part of why specialty Arabica works better for this use case than instant, which can vary significantly between servings.
Can I drink cold brew before a workout instead?+
Cold brew has high caffeine, but the larger liquid volume and slower caffeine release rate make it a sustained-release format — better for a long morning focus session than a sharp pre-workout window. If you prefer the flavour, cold brew will still help. But for timing the caffeine peak to your first working set, espresso is more precise.
Why is cold brew better post-workout?+
Cold brew is naturally lower in acidity than hot espresso — roughly 60–70% lower. After a session, especially fasted training, the stomach has been under physical stress. Cold brew delivers the caffeine you need for the work day ahead without the acidity that can cause discomfort in the two hours following exercise.
Will coffee dehydrate me at the gym?+
The diuretic effect of moderate caffeine — one to two cups — is mild and well within normal hydration management. Research on caffeine and dehydration in exercising adults shows it does not meaningfully impact hydration status at the doses we are talking about. Drink water during your session as normal.
What about mixing coffee with a protein shake?+
Nothing wrong with this — iced espresso over a protein shake is a common enough combination. Caffeine and protein do not interact negatively. The main consideration is total liquid volume: if you want the caffeine to peak quickly, a smaller espresso shot works faster than a large milk-based coffee drink.
