Section 01 · The reality today

The co-working coffee situation in Ahmedabad, as it stands

Ahmedabad's co-working market has grown fast. SaltCube, Regus, WeWork-style formats, and dozens of independent operators now serve the city's consultants, remote employees, startup teams, and freelancers. The infrastructure is genuinely good — fast WiFi, ergonomic seating, meeting rooms on demand, rooftop terraces. The coffee is not keeping pace with any of it.

Walk into most of these spaces and your options are: a vending machine dispensing instant powder in hot water, a chai pantry with UHT milk, or a Google Maps detour to the nearest cafe that may or may not be open and is almost certainly a 10-minute round trip. For a professional who is mid-meeting, on a deadline, or simply trying to stay focused, none of these are the answer.

This is not an Ahmedabad-specific failure. Co-working coffee is mediocre across India. But Ahmedabad is the city where we are starting — and the gap here is particularly sharp. The professionals inside these spaces are, in many cases, the most coffee-aware segment of the population. They have travelled, they drink specialty coffee when they visit Mumbai or Bengaluru, and they are paying ₹8,000–15,000 per desk per month for a premium workspace experience. The coffee is the one thing the space cannot seem to get right.

Section 02 · Why this audience is different

Why the co-working professional is Ahmedabad's most important coffee consumer

Coffee consumption follows a simple pattern in most settings: one cup at breakfast, maybe one in the afternoon. Co-working professionals break this pattern. Their workday is longer, more cognitively demanding, and less structured than an office environment with fixed breaks. They drink coffee to start the day, again during a 10am call, again after lunch when the focus dips, and sometimes once more before an evening deadline.

That is two to four cups a day, five days a week, every working week of the year. For a 300-person co-working space, that is a minimum of 600 cups on a conservative day — likely closer to 900 on a busy one. The demand is not theoretical. It is already happening. It is just being served by a machine that costs ₹15 per cup and produces something that tastes like it.

Section 03 · Why current options fail

The three options that co-working spaces currently offer — and what is wrong with each

Option 1: The vending machine

The standard co-working space coffee solution. Pre-mixed Robusta powder, hot water, paper cup. Caffeine content is inconsistent. Flavour is bitter and flat. The machine breaks every few weeks and takes three days to get serviced. It is there because it is cheap to install and requires zero operational management. It has nothing to do with what the people inside the space actually want from coffee.

Option 2: The chai pantry

Some spaces have a staffed pantry offering chai, instant coffee, and UHT milk. This is better than the vending machine in terms of human involvement, but the output is broadly the same. The chai is fine — chai is hard to get wrong at scale. The coffee is not fine, because instant coffee made with UHT milk is not coffee in any meaningful sense. The pantry exists to check a box, not to serve the actual demand in the room.

Option 3: Leave the building

This is the de facto solution for anyone who actually cares about what they drink. Find the nearest specialty cafe — in Ahmedabad, that might be a 5–15 minute walk or drive depending on location — join the queue, order, wait, walk back. By the time you are back at your desk, 20 minutes of your workday has gone to getting a coffee. That is not a sustainable daily habit for anyone on a deadline.

Why is vending machine coffee so consistently bad?+
Vending machine coffee typically uses pre-mixed Robusta powder — cheaper, more bitter, and with more caffeine variation than freshly ground Arabica. The hot water extraction is neither espresso nor filter — it falls somewhere between the two and does neither well. The machines are also rarely cleaned to the standard that a professional machine requires, which compounds the quality problem over time.
Will the P.S. Pass work for co-working professionals specifically?+
The P.S. Pass is designed for exactly this use case. You buy a bundle of cups once — 10 or 20 at a time — and draw down from that balance every time you order. No payment friction per transaction, no daily decision about whether to spend money. For a co-working professional who is in the same building five days a week, this removes the last remaining obstacle to making specialty coffee a genuine daily habit.
What makes specialty coffee better for productivity than instant?+
Two factors: caffeine consistency and absence of additives. 100% Arabica espresso delivers a predictable caffeine range with a cleaner rise and fall than Robusta-heavy instant blends. The absence of artificial flavouring, fillers, and pre-mixed milk means what you are consuming is cleaner. Over a full workday with two or three cups, that consistency adds up — you are not riding a caffeine spike from an overdosed vending machine serving followed by a longer-than-expected crash.
Does P.S. Coffee plan to serve multiple floors or zones within a large co-working space?+
The Pod format is sized for a single anchor point within a space — typically the lobby, reception area, or pantry zone. For large multi-floor co-working buildings, the app ordering model means members on any floor can place an order and collect it at a single Pod without the round trip being prohibitive. For very large campuses, we will evaluate multi-Pod installations as the format scales.
