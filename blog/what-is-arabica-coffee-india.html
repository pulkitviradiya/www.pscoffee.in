The quick answer

Arabica coffee (Coffea arabica) is one of the two main species of coffee bean, accounting for about 60% of global production. It grows at higher altitudes (900–2,000 m), tastes smoother and sweeter with natural fruit and chocolate notes, and contains roughly half the caffeine of Robusta. When a coffee brand says '100% Arabica,' it means the cup contains no Robusta, no chicory, and no filler only the higher-grade bean. P.S. Coffee will use 100% Indian Arabica, sourced locally from Chikmagalur and Coorg.

Key takeaways

Arabica is one of two main coffee species the other is Robusta. Arabica is ~60% of global production.

Arabica grows at 900–2,000m altitude; Robusta below 900m. Altitude is what creates the flavour complexity.

Arabica tastes smoother, sweeter, more complex fruit, chocolate, and nutty notes. Robusta is harsher and more bitter.

Indian Arabica from Chikmagalur and Coorg is world-class shade-grown alongside cardamom and pepper for unique spice notes.

Most QSR coffee in India is a 60/40 Arabica-Robusta blend. P.S. Coffee will use 100% Arabica only.

It takes 3–5 cups for your palate to fully appreciate the difference by cup 5, blended coffee will taste harsh.

Section 01 · Species

Arabica vs Robusta the practical difference in your cup

Robusta is cheaper, stronger, more bitter, and produces a thicker crema. It is used in most instant coffees, budget espresso blends, and South Indian filter coffee mixes. Arabica is more expensive, harder to grow, and produces a significantly more complex, smoother cup.

When you taste a coffee and notice notes of chocolate, citrus, or nuts that is Arabica's flavour doing its job. When you taste flat bitterness and a gritty aftertaste that is Robusta working as designed, for low cost and high volume.

Section 02 · Origin

India grows some of the world's best Arabica

Most people do not know this. Indian Arabica from Chikmagalur in Karnataka and Coorg grows at 900–1,800 metres, shade-grown alongside spices like cardamom and pepper. This intercropping adds subtle spice notes that are genuinely unique to Indian-origin coffee you cannot grow this anywhere else, because no one else grows coffee next to cardamom at altitude.

India exports premium Arabica to European specialty roasters. Then, in a strange round trip, the country imports blended instant coffee powder made by the same companies. The best Indian Arabica is shipped out. The everyday Indian cup is mostly Robusta.

Section 03 · Why 100% matters

Why "100% Arabica" matters in a grab-and-go format

Most QSR coffee in India uses a blend typically 60% Arabica, 40% Robusta because it reduces the cost per cup by ₹8–12. The Robusta adds bitterness, weight, and a thicker crema. Many customers read that bitterness as "strength" and assume it is a quality signal. It is the opposite.

P.S. Coffee is being built without that blend. The cup will be 100% Arabica. No exceptions. No "premium tier" upcharge for it.

The cost savings that make ₹89 specialty coffee possible will not come from the bean. They will come from the Pod format lower rent, lower labour overhead, no ambiance investment. The overhead is what we are lowering. The bean is what we are protecting. This is the entire quality promise of P.S. Coffee in one ingredient decision.

Section 04 · Palate

How to taste the difference (it's subtle at first)

If you have spent years drinking blended instant coffee or budget espresso, your first 100% Arabica cup may feel strange lighter, less aggressive, more complex. Your palate is used to Robusta's blunt bitterness, and Arabica does not deliver that. It delivers something subtler.

By the third or fourth cup, you will notice:

  • Smoothness. No harsh aftertaste. No grit on the tongue.
  • Natural sweetness. A subtle caramel, chocolate, or nutty note not added, inherent to the bean.
  • No spike-and-crash. Lower caffeine means steadier energy, not a 90-minute peak followed by a slump.
  • Complexity. Different notes on different roast days, because the bean is alive enough to vary.

This is not a marketing line. It is a palate adjustment. Once your tongue learns Arabica, you cannot un-taste the harshness in everything else.

Frequently asked questions

What does "100% Arabica" actually mean?+
It means every bean in the cup is from the Arabica species (Coffea arabica). No blending with Robusta, no chicory, no fillers. It is a purity standard, not a marketing label.
Why do most coffee brands use a blend instead of 100% Arabica?+
Cost. Robusta is significantly cheaper (₹180–250/kg vs ₹400–600/kg for Arabica). A 40% Robusta blend reduces the cost per cup by ₹8–12, which becomes ₹2–3 crore annually at a 100-outlet scale.
Is Indian Arabica really comparable to African or South American Arabica?+
Yes. Indian Arabica from Chikmagalur and Coorg grows at 900–1,800 metres with shade-cropping that adds unique spice notes from neighbouring cardamom and pepper plants. European specialty roasters actively buy Indian beans the difference is mostly that Indians export the best and import the blended.
How long until I can taste the difference?+
Usually three to five cups. The first cup may feel 'lighter' because your palate expects Robusta's bitterness. By the fifth, you will register the smoothness and natural sweetness and blended coffee will start tasting harsh by comparison.
Will P.S. Coffee really stay 100% Arabica even at scale?+
Yes. The cost savings the brand depends on are coming from the Pod format, not from cutting the bean. Switching to a Robusta blend at scale would break the entire quality promise and the promise is the brand.
Does 100% Arabica have less caffeine?+
Yes about half. Arabica is 1.2–1.5% caffeine by weight, Robusta is 2.0–2.7%. That's why Arabica is smoother and less jittery, while Robusta is sometimes used in commercial espresso blends specifically to boost the caffeine punch.
